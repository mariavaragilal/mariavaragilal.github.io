# Who I am as a designer

I'm a designer. My foundation is design. The tools change — Figma, React, Gatsby, a brand, a campaign — but the thinking doesn't.

I don't design in code. I design where the design has to survive. Sometimes that's a React component. Sometimes it's a brand system. Sometimes it's a landing page for a campaign. The medium depends on where the work needs to live to stay real — code is one of those places, not the only one.

## How I work

I start with the brief. Before I open any tool, I sit with the problem and analyse it. What's missing? What will break for someone who isn't like me? Where are the pain points that nobody flagged because they seem too small or too obvious?

That's where the real design happens — in the analysis, before anything is visual. By the time I'm building, the hard decisions are already made. The interface is the consequence of those decisions, not the starting point.

I work within teams — backend engineers, support, product. My role is to be the layer that reduces translation loss: the thinking that's clear in design doesn't degrade by the time it reaches code, and the constraints that exist in engineering don't get ignored in design. That gap is where most products quietly break. Closing it is the work.

I have a simple test for whether something works: could my parents use it? Could my grandparents? Not because I'm designing for seniors specifically, but because if someone from a different generation, with no context and no patience for learning curves, can navigate what I built without me having to explain it — that's when I know the design is doing its job. Accessibility isn't a checklist I apply at the end. It's how I think from the start. Every generation, every context, every level of familiarity.

## The loop

I anticipate first. During the brief and analysis phase, I identify the pain points that would normally be missed and address them in the next phase. That's the proactive part.

Then I ship. And I watch. If something I designed creates friction I didn't foresee, I change it. I'm not precious about my own decisions — if the walkthrough I designed creates pressure instead of trust, it goes. That's the production-informed part.

Anticipate, ship, observe, refine. Both halves matter. The proactive instinct catches most of it. The production loop catches the rest.

## What I actually do

I own the design and frontend layer. Research, flows, UI — and when the direction is set, I build it: components, interactions, API integration, the pieces that make the designed experience real in production. I work alongside engineers, and the handoff runs in every direction — the project decides who leads and when. When it happens, I make it frictionless because I speak both languages. Not because I'm a developer who designs, but because I'm a designer who refuses to let the work degrade between intention and delivery.

When I write a React component, I'm making design decisions. When I choose a styling foundation — Bootstrap, Tailwind, MUI — I'm deciding what the team builds on. When I keep a disabled option visible instead of hiding it, that's a UX decision about honesty. The code is the medium. The design is the thinking.

I've spent the last decade in one ecosystem — working with backend engineers and product teams to design and build the brand identity, the corporate site, the product UIs, the auth layer, the account hub, and the shared styling foundation. Six products, one identity. That's not maintenance work — that's systems design at a scale where every new feature is a potential drift point and every decision has to hold across the entire platform.

## What I bring

I see what the spec didn't cover. I question assumptions during the brief, not after launch. And I build the frontend layer so the gap between what was intended and what shipped stays as close to zero as I can keep it.

The work has always been about one thing: making complex systems feel simple for the person using them, no matter who they are.
